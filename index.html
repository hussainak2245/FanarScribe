01 / Problem

Documentation is stealing time from care.

Outpatient clinicians document after every visit while working through high patient flow, limited time, Arabic dialects, English medical terms, and informal symptom descriptions.

Burden

Clinical notes, summaries, forms, and instructions stretch the consultation beyond the room.

Language

Patients move between Arabic dialects, accents, and English terminology in one visit.

Risk

A polished AI note can hide uncertainty unless the clinician is asked to confirm.

02 / Market

Qatar is not a blank market. It is a concentrated launch point.

The opportunity is not to prove that clinical AI can exist. The opportunity is to turn existing momentum into a product that fits local outpatient care.

Global pull

AI scribes are already a category.

DAX and Scribeberry show clear demand for ambient notes, summaries, and physician-facing documentation UX.

Regional momentum

Health AI is moving from idea to infrastructure.

Avey-style medical APIs and Arabic health-AI startups show that the region is ready for specialized clinical tools.

Qatar fit

Primary care is dense, multilingual, and workflow-heavy.

PHCC gives the use case a focused surface: busy clinics, many clinicians, and patients moving across languages.

SAJIL's wedge

A clinician-facing layer that connects Fanar, Arabic speech understanding, medical retrieval, and outpatient documentation into one usable workflow.

03 / Landscape

Transcription is solved enough. Clinical reliability is not.

Current scribes can transcribe, summarize, and format. The harder product problem is uncertainty, hallucination control, consent, privacy, dialect handling, and physician review.

English-first scribes

Strong documentation UX, ambient notes, and structured summaries.

Regional AI pieces

Avey-style health APIs, QCRI Arabic speech research, and Fanar's Arabic-first direction.

SAJIL gap

One clinician-facing workflow for Arabic consultation intelligence in Qatar.

04 / Solution

A clinical co-pilot that keeps the doctor in control.

SAJIL captures consultation input, generates structured notes, flags unclear details, retrieves trusted context, and asks the clinician to confirm before finalizing.

01 Listen

Arabic, Gulf dialect, and mixed medical speech.

02 Structure

History, symptoms, assessment, plan, and instructions.

03 Ground

RAG over approved guidelines and client documents.

04 Ask

Uncertainty checkpoints before the final note.
